Three responses to this moment
✗

Ban it.

Schools that go this route discover within weeks that the policy changes nothing. Students use AI on personal devices, at home, in study halls. You have added a rule without addressing the reality.

✗

Ignore it.

Each passing semester this position becomes harder to hold. The research is mainstream. Parents are asking. Faculty are frustrated. Every month you wait, the gap widens between what your school promises and what your graduates can do without AI assistance.

→

Design it.

Choose what kind of AI gets into your school. Configure it for learning rather than one that replaces it. Build something your faculty can explain to parents, your advisors can use to know their students better, and your board can stand behind. This is the only option that treats the problem as what it is.

The Third Option

Orchen is what designing it without gaps looks like.

Every architectural decision in Orchen follows from one conviction: learning happens in the space between not knowing and knowing. An AI that removes this space does not help students learn more efficiently. It removes the thing that learning requires. Orchen's AI asks before it answers. It requires students to articulate their thinking before receiving any response. It treats each conversation as an opportunity to develop independent reasoning, not to circumvent it.

That conviction plays out across three concrete layers.

Three layers.
One package.

01
Layer One

The Device

Pre-configured. Pre-enrolled. Arriving ready.

A MacBook Neo, enrolled in your MDM profile before it ships and arriving ready from day one. Competing AI tools are blocked at both the device and DNS level via Jamf and Cloudflare. The only AI environment available on the device is yours. No IT setup required. No policy document that students work around.

02
Layer Two

The AI Environment

Socratic by design. Configured for the individual.

A school-specific AI tutor built with a custom Socratic system prompt that grows and adapts with each student. The AI asks before it answers — requiring students to explain where they are stuck, identify what they already understand, and work through problems rather than around them. It is configured around your school's identity, your faculty's pedagogical preferences, and your institutional voice. It is not a generic AI with a disclaimer added.
